Pink Wave

A look into the women who won in the 2018 midterm elections.

A project by Madison Hindo

A Visual Overview

This map provides a visual overview of the places where 116 women won their congressional elections in the 2018 midterm elections.

Click on the markers to learn the names of the winning female candidates, their party identification, and what district they represent!

Groundbreaking Women

Click on the photographs below to learn more about some of the women who made history with their victories this election cycle.

Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez is the youngest woman elected to Congress and will represent New York’s 14th congressional district, which covers parts of the Bronx and Queens. She ran on a platform of Medicare-for-All, a Green New Deal, and a Federal Jobs Guarantee. She has degrees in economics and international relations, and her background is in community organizing. Ocasio-Cortez defeated ten-term Representative Joe Crowley in the primary, which was considered the biggest upset in the 2018 primaries. Learn more about Ocasio-Cortez here.

Along with Rashida Tlaib, Ilhan Omar is the first Muslim women to serve in Congress. She is from Somalia and will be the first Somali American to be elected to Congress. She is also the first woman of color to represent Minnesota in the House of Representatives. In 2016, she was elected to the Minnesota House of Representatives where she serves as the Assistant Minority Leader. She has experience with policy analysis, nonprofit work, and community organizing. Omar campaigned on Medicare for All, education, and immigration reform. Learn more about Omar here.

Ayanna Pressley was the first woman of color elected to the Boston City Council in 2009, and she will now be the first black woman to represent Massachusetts in Congress. Before serving on the Boston City Council, Pressley served as a Senior Aide to both Joseph P. Kennedy and John Kerry. She defeated Michael Capuano with a surprise win in the primary with 59 percent of the vote. Pressley campaigned on the issues of economic development, public health, and the environment. Learn more about Pressley here.

Kyrsten Sinema was narrowly elected to be an Arizona senator, and she will be both the first female senator from the state and the first openly bisexual person to be elected to the Senate. She was first elected to the Arizona Legislature since 2004 and has served in the U.S. House of Representatives since 2012. She has been described as a centrist or moderate Democrat who campaigned on breaking gridlock in D.C., affordable health care, and supporting veterans. Learn more about Sinema here.

Get Involved!

Reproductive rights involve a woman’s choice to decide whether and when to have children. They are important for many reasons such as socioeconomic well-being and health. This encompasses access to safe abortions, contraceptives, and unbiased information.

If you want to help protect reproductive rights, consider donating to the Center for Reproductive Rights. CRR is a nonprofit that uses legal advocacy to fight for stronger laws protecting reproductive health autonomy in the United States and around the world.

Consider volunteering with Planned Parenthood (or donating!). As a volunteer, you can phone bank, escort patients into the clinic, and participate in community outreach events. Sign up here!

The National Domestic Violence defines domestic violence as “a pattern of behaviors used by one partner to maintain power and control over another partner in an intimate relationship.” It includes physical violence, emotional abuse, and sexual violence, and it can vary in severity and frequency. To learn more, click here.

Consider donating to support the National Domestic Violence Hotline! The Hotline is confidential and operates 24/7 to provide support to victims of domestic violence.

If you live in or near Alachua, Bradford, or Union counties, consider volunteering with Peaceful Paths. Peaceful Paths is a domestic violence shelter, and volunteers can do administrative work or work directly with victims.

Women make up 51 percent of the United States, yet they make up only 23.3 percent of Congress. That number is increasing, but there is still a long way to go until we reach parity. Women have a stake in the future of this country, and they deserve a say in how it’s run.

Whether you’re considering running yourself or want to empower other women to run, check out EMILY’S List! This organization recruits and endorses pro-choice Democratic female candidates at all levels of government and gives them the resources they need to help win an election. If you can’t donate or aren’t interested in running, considering volunteering for an EMILY’S List endorsed candidate near you!

If you want to help out candidates specifically in Florida, consider volunteering with Ruth’s List. The goals of these organization is the same, but Ruth’s List is focused on Florida while EMILY’S List is a national organization.
